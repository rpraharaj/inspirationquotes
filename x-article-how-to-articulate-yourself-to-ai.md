## The Three Levels of Prompt Articulation

I've studied the best vibe coders I know. The ones building real products. Getting funding. Making money.

They all think about prompts the same way.

Three levels. Master these, you're in the 1%.

### Level 1: The Micro Story Prompt

Most people describe features. Winners describe transformations.

**Loser prompt:**  
"Make a login page"

**Winner prompt:**  
"Right now, users land on an ugly form and immediately bounce. Build a dark-themed login that feels premium—like they're accessing something exclusive. Email, password, smooth animations on focus."

**Why it works:**

Problem → Stakes → Solution.

This is how humans communicate insights. Turns out, it's how AI understands them too.

You're not just describing what you want. You're describing why it matters and what changes.

The AI doesn't need a feature list. It needs context.

---

### Level 2: The Pyramid Prompt

Answer first. Details second.

**Bad:**  
"I need some kind of dashboard thing with feedback and maybe analytics..."

**Good:**  
"Build a customer feedback dashboard.

Core features:
- Submission form (5-star rating + text comment)
- Filterable list of all feedback entries
- Analytics summary showing average rating
- Persistent storage using local storage

Design: Minimal, blue accents, mobile-first, dark mode."

**The pattern:**

Main conclusion → Supporting arguments → Evidence.

Same structure McKinsey consultants use. Same structure that works with AI.

Clarity is universal.

When you lead with the goal, the AI knows where you're going from sentence one. Every detail that follows makes sense in context.

When you ramble and hope the AI figures it out? You get rambling outputs.

---

### Level 3: The Cross-Context Synthesis (Where Non-Techies Win)

Here's where domain experts destroy developers.

**Developer prompt:**  
"Build a workout tracking app with sets, reps, and weight fields."

**Domain expert prompt:**  
"I train gym beginners who feel intimidated. Traditional fitness apps are overwhelming—too much jargon, too many metrics. This needs to feel like a supportive friend. Celebrate small wins. No shame. Think Duolingo's encouragement applied to fitness. When someone logs a workout, show confetti and a message like 'That's 3 days in a row—you're unstoppable!'"

**The insight:**

Developers know code. Domain experts know humans.

AI bridges the gap.

Your expertise in *your field* becomes your unfair advantage. The AI handles the rest.

This is why teachers, designers, and marketers often build better AI products than engineers.

They understand the problem deeply. The AI just needs to understand their explanation.

Don't apologize for not being technical. Your domain knowledge IS your technical advantage now.

## The Secret Nobody Told Me

I'll be honest—I sucked at this for months.

I'm technical. I understand code. But my prompts were trash.

Why? Because I was describing *how* to build things instead of *what* I wanted.

Then I watched a non-technical founder build a better app than me in half the time.

His secret? He treated AI like a talented intern.

He didn't explain implementation. He explained outcomes.

"When a user clicks here, they should feel X. The flow should accomplish Y. The design should communicate Z."

Outcomes. Not instructions.

Here's my rule now:

**Before writing any prompt, I ask myself:**
- What is the user experiencing?
- What should they feel?
- What's the transformation?

If I can't answer these, I don't write the prompt yet.

Because unclear thinking produces unclear results. Every. Single. Time.

The best prompt isn't the most detailed.

It's the most *clear*.

## The Only Practice That Matters

Forget courses. Forget tools. Here's the only practice that compounds:

### The Daily Articulation Practice

**Every day for 30 days:**

1. Build something small (a button, a form, a page)
2. Before prompting the AI, write down:
   - What is this?
   - Who is it for?
   - What should it feel like?
3. If you struggle to answer, you're not ready to prompt
4. Write the prompt
5. Review the output
6. Save prompts that worked
7. Delete prompts that didn't

**Why this compounds:**

After 30 days, you'll have:
- A library of proven prompt patterns
- Muscle memory for clear articulation  
- Intuition for what works

This becomes automatic. You'll think in prompts.

### Your Greatest Hits Album

Master these 5 core prompts, and you can build anything:

1. **The Blueprint** - Describe complete applications from scratch
2. **The Iterator** - "Keep X, change Y to Z"
3. **The Debugger** - "This breaks when [scenario], fix it by [approach]"
4. **The Reference** - "Like [app name] but for [my use case]"
5. **The Explainer** - "Walk me through why this approach works"

That's it. Five patterns. Infinite results.

You don't need 100 prompting tricks. You need 5 patterns you've practiced 100 times.
